Berthold Woltze’s The Irritating Gentleman (Der lästige Herr), painted in 1874, is a quietly comic genre scene that captures a moment of social discomfort with precise observation and gentle satire. Woltze depicts a well-dressed, overly familiar man leaning into the personal space of a seated woman who reacts with a mixture of annoyance and restrained politeness; the artist renders their expressions and body language with sympathetic detail, turning a minor social transgression into a vivid character study. The muted, naturalistic palette, careful handling of light, and unobtrusive background keep the focus on the interplay between the two figures, while subtle details—folded gloves, the gentleman’s tilted hat, the woman’s averted gaze—convey class, manners, and the gendered expectations of late 19th-century bourgeois society. The painting’s blend of humor and critique exemplifies Woltze’s skill at transforming everyday situations into telling commentary, making The Irritating Gentleman his most widely recognized and enduring work.
